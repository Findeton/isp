Author: Felix Robles Elvira

Status: first investigation draft. Paper 13 starts from the Paper-12
finite-battery renormalized unsmeared Wilson-loop bridge and asks whether it
can be extended to a dense operational loop algebra in a way that deserves
the name "continuum Yang-Mills functional."

The Barandes-aligned rule is strict: the loop algebra is an operational
record algebra. Its elements are declared Wilson-loop tests, finite products,
and limits of such tests. No field, Hilbert space, local net, or Markovian
partial-kernel factorization is fundamental in this paper. Those structures
may appear only as representations reconstructed after positivity,
covariance, continuity, and whole-process consistency have been proved.

## 2. Operational Dense Loop Algebra

### Definition 2.1: Admissible Completion

Let `{\mathcal L}_{\rm adm}` be the union of the admissible strata of Paper
12, each equipped with `d_{\rm adm}`. The admissible completion
`\overline{\mathcal L}_{\rm adm}` is the metric completion after identifying
loops whose Wilson-loop records agree on every finite Paper-12 battery.

This is not the space of all rough loops. It is the closure of the declared
operational loop class under the topology that Paper 12 actually controls.

### Definition 2.2: Countable Dense Loop Skeleton

A countable dense loop skeleton is a sequence

```math
{\mathcal D}_1\subset{\mathcal D}_2\subset\cdots
\subset{\mathcal L}_{\rm adm}
```

such that:

1. each `{\mathcal D}_n` is finite;
2. each loop in `{\mathcal D}_n` has rational piecewise-polynomial
   parametrization, rational cusp parameters, and representation labels from
   a finite representation set `{\mathcal R}_n`;
3. `\bigcup_n{\mathcal D}_n` is dense in
   `\overline{\mathcal L}_{\rm adm}`;
4. every finite Paper-12 battery is contained in some `{\mathcal D}_n`
   after approximating loops inside its admissible stratum.

### Lemma 2.2A: Dense Skeleton Exists On The Admissible Completion

Every Paper-12 admissible loop completion admits a countable dense loop
skeleton.

### Proof

On a fixed admissible stratum, loops have bounded length, bounded total
curvature, finitely many labeled cusps, cusp angles bounded away from
degeneracy, and nonadjacent arcs separated by `r_min`. Piecewise-polynomial
constant-speed parametrizations with rational coefficients are dense in the
`C^1` part of `d_{\rm adm}`; rational cusp parameters and rational cusp
angles are dense in the compact allowed cusp-angle interval. Taking rational
approximants that preserve the separation margins after a sufficiently small
perturbation gives a countable dense subset of each stratum. The countable
union over rational bounds
`L_max,N_max,theta_min^{-1},r_min^{-1},K_max` and finite representation
cutoffs gives the desired skeleton. `square`

### Definition 2.3: Operational Loop Algebra

For a finite skeleton `{\mathcal D}_n`, let `{\mathcal A}_n` be the unital
commutative real algebra generated by symbols

```math
W_{\rho}^{\rm ren}(C),
\qquad
C\in{\mathcal D}_n,\quad \rho\in{\mathcal R}_n,
```

and finite products up to the declared battery degree. The involution is
trivial on real scalar Wilson-loop records. The countable operational loop
algebra is

```math
{\mathcal A}_0
=
\bigcup_{n\ge1}{\mathcal A}_n.
```

The algebra is an algebra of records. It is not assumed to be an algebra of
fundamental field operators.

## 3. Projective Whole-Process Consistency

### Definition 3.1: Finite-Battery Functional

A Paper-12 finite-battery functional on `{\mathcal A}_n` is a linear map

```math
S_n:{\mathcal A}_n\to{\mathbb R}
```

obtained as the renormalized unsmeared continuum limit of the corresponding
finite battery along the declared whole-process refinement trajectory.

It is admissible when it is:

1. normalized: `S_n(1)=1`;
2. product-bounded on `{\mathcal A}_n`;
3. reflection-positive on positive-time subalgebras;
4. Euclidean covariant up to the finite lattice symmetry approximation;
5. continuous on the finite loop metric data inherited from Paper 12.

### Definition 3.2: Projective Consistency

The family `{S_n}` is projectively consistent if, whenever
`{\mathcal A}_n\subset{\mathcal A}_m`,

```math
S_m|_{{\mathcal A}_n}=S_n.
```

It is approximately projectively consistent if there is a defect
`\delta_{n,m}` such that

```math
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_m(F)-S_n(F)|
\le
\delta_{n,m},
\qquad
\lim_{m\to\infty}\delta_{n,m}=0
```

for each fixed `n`. The norm `\|\cdot\|_n` is the finite battery norm defined
by the product/cumulant envelope constants.

Approximate consistency is the honest form expected from a growing
finite-battery RG ledger. It is still whole-process consistency, not
composition of partial kernels.

### Theorem 3.3: Projective Consistency Gives A Countable Functional

If `{S_n}` is projectively consistent, then there is a unique linear
functional

```math
S_0:{\mathcal A}_0\to{\mathbb R}
```

such that `S_0|_{{\mathcal A}_n}=S_n` for every `n`.

If `{S_n}` is approximately projectively consistent and Cauchy on every fixed
`{\mathcal A}_n`, the same conclusion holds with

```math
S_0(F)=\lim_{m\to\infty}S_m(F)
```

for `F\in{\mathcal A}_n`.

### Proof

In the exact case, define `S_0(F)=S_n(F)` for any `n` with
`F\in{\mathcal A}_n`. The definition is independent of `n` by projective
consistency. Linearity is inherited from the finite stages.

In the approximate case, the Cauchy condition makes `S_m(F)` converge for
each fixed `F`, and the limit is independent of the finite stage containing
`F`. `square`

### Theorem 3.4: Summable Inclusion Defects Give Projective Consistency

Let `{\mathcal A}_1\subset{\mathcal A}_2\subset\cdots` be the operational
loop skeleton algebras. Suppose there are inclusion defects `\eta_n` with

```math
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_{n+1}(F)-S_n(F)|
\le
\eta_n,
\qquad
\sum_{n\ge1}\eta_n<\infty.
```

Then `{S_n}` is Cauchy on every fixed finite algebra and therefore defines a
countable functional by Theorem 3.3.

### Proof

For `m>n` and `F\in{\mathcal A}_n` with `\|F\|_n\le1`,

```math
|S_m(F)-S_n(F)|
\le
\sum_{k=n}^{m-1}|S_{k+1}(F)-S_k(F)|
\le
\sum_{k=n}^{m-1}\eta_k.
```

The tail of a summable series tends to zero, so `S_m(F)` is Cauchy. Scaling
handles arbitrary `F`. Theorem 3.3 gives the countable functional. `square`

The defects `\eta_n` are whole-process finite-battery comparison defects.
They are not transition probabilities between intermediate times.

## 4. Dense Continuity Extension

### Definition 4.1: Algebraic Loop Modulus

The countable functional `S_0` has an algebraic loop modulus if, for every
finite product degree and representation cutoff, there exists
`\omega_N(\delta)\downarrow0` such that replacing each loop in a product by a
loop within `d_{\rm adm}`-distance at most `\delta` changes the value of
`S_0` by at most `\omega_N(\delta)`.

### Theorem 4.2: Dense Loop Extension

Assume:

1. `{S_n}` gives a countable functional `S_0` by Theorem 3.3;
2. `S_0` has an algebraic loop modulus;
3. the finite-stage product bounds are locally uniform on admissible strata.

Then `S_0` extends uniquely to a continuous functional

```math
S:{\mathcal A}_{\rm adm}\to{\mathbb R}
```

on the completion of the operational loop algebra generated by
`\overline{\mathcal L}_{\rm adm}`.

### Proof

Let `F_k` be a Cauchy sequence of algebraic loop records in the completion.
The algebraic loop modulus makes `S_0(F_k)` a Cauchy sequence in
`{\mathbb R}`. Define `S(F)=\lim_kS_0(F_k)`. If two Cauchy sequences represent
the same completed record, their mutual distance tends to zero, so the
modulus forces the same value. Linearity follows by passing to limits.
Uniqueness follows because the skeleton is dense. `square`

## 5. Positivity And Euclidean Covariance In The Extension

### Theorem 5.1: Reflection Positivity Extends From Finite Batteries

Assume every finite `S_n` is reflection-positive on its positive-time
subalgebra and the algebraic loop modulus is compatible with reflection.
Then the completed functional `S` is reflection-positive on the completed
positive-time operational algebra.

### Proof

For any completed positive-time record `F`, choose finite skeleton
approximants `F_k`. Reflection positivity gives

```math
S_0(F_k\vartheta F_k)\ge0.
```

Continuity of multiplication on the declared finite-product completion and
the algebraic loop modulus imply
`S_0(F_k\vartheta F_k)\to S(F\vartheta F)`. Hence
`S(F\vartheta F)\ge0`. `square`

### Theorem 5.2: Euclidean Covariance Extends From Finite Batteries

Assume finite-stage Euclidean covariance defects vanish and the approximant
rule is equivariant in the completed admissible topology. Then the completed
functional `S` is Euclidean covariant:

```math
S(gF)=S(F)
```

for every admissible Euclidean motion `g` preserving the loop completion.

### Proof

Approximate `F` and `gF` by skeleton records. Finite-stage covariance gives
equality up to a defect tending to zero. The loop modulus removes the
remaining approximation error. `square`

## 6. Regulator And Gauge-Chart Independence

### Definition 6.1: Whole-Process Regulator Equivalence

Two refinement trajectories `\mathfrak R` and `\mathfrak R'` are equivalent
on the dense loop algebra if their finite-battery functionals satisfy

```math
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_n^{\mathfrak R}(F)-S_n^{\mathfrak R'}(F)|
\le
\Delta_n(\mathfrak R,\mathfrak R'),
\qquad
\Delta_n(\mathfrak R,\mathfrak R')\to0
```

for every fixed finite battery.

This is not a comparison of partial transition kernels. It is a comparison of
whole-process record functionals on declared batteries.

### Theorem 6.2: Regulator Equivalence Gives A Unique Functional

If two refinement trajectories are equivalent in the sense of Definition 6.1
and both satisfy the hypotheses of Theorem 4.2, then they define the same
completed loop functional `S`.

### Proof

For every skeleton record `F`, the finite-battery values agree in the limit.
By density and continuity, the completed functionals agree everywhere.
`square`

### Definition 6.3: Common-Refinement Ledger

Two whole-process refinement trajectories `\mathfrak R` and `\mathfrak R'`
admit a common-refinement ledger on a finite battery `{\mathcal A}_n` if
there is a third declared whole-process trajectory `\mathfrak R''` and
finite-battery comparison defects

```math
\Delta_{n,k}(\mathfrak R,\mathfrak R''),
\qquad
\Delta_{n,k}(\mathfrak R',\mathfrak R'')
```

such that, at refinement depth `k`,

```math
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak R}(F)-S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak R''}(F)|
\le
\Delta_{n,k}(\mathfrak R,\mathfrak R''),
```

and the analogous estimate holds for `\mathfrak R'`, with both defects
tending to zero as `k\to\infty`.

The common refinement is a comparison of complete record functionals on the
same finite battery. It is not an assertion that either trajectory factors
into Markovian subprocesses.

### Theorem 6.4: Common Refinement Implies Regulator Equivalence

If every pair of admissible regulators has a common-refinement ledger on each
finite battery, then the regulators are equivalent in the sense of Definition
6.1.

### Proof

For `F\in{\mathcal A}_n` with `\|F\|_n\le1`,

```math
|S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak R}(F)-S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak R'}(F)|
\le
\Delta_{n,k}(\mathfrak R,\mathfrak R'')
+
\Delta_{n,k}(\mathfrak R',\mathfrak R'').
```

Taking `k\to\infty` gives zero. Thus the limiting finite-battery functionals
agree for each fixed `n`; Definition 6.1 follows. `square`

### Definition 6.5: Gauge-Chart Transition Defect

Let `\chi` and `\chi'` be two gauge-fixing or block-coordinate charts used to
write the same whole-process gauge-orbit integral. The chart transition
defect on `{\mathcal A}_n` is

```math
\Theta_{n,k}(\chi,\chi')
=
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_{n,k}^{\chi}(F)-S_{n,k}^{\chi'}(F)|.
```

The defect is admissible if

```math
\Theta_{n,k}(\chi,\chi')\to0
```

for every fixed finite battery.

### Theorem 6.6: Gauge-Chart Independence For Scalar Loop Records

Assume:

1. the scalar Wilson-loop records in `{\mathcal A}_n` are gauge-invariant
   closed-trace records;
2. chart changes are changes of coordinates on the same declared gauge-orbit
   integral;
3. Jacobian, boundary, residual-root-gauge, and local-truncation errors are
   all included in the chart transition defect `\Theta_{n,k}`;
4. `\Theta_{n,k}\to0` for every fixed finite battery.

Then the completed loop functional is independent of the gauge chart.

### Proof

If the chart change were exact, gauge-invariant closed-loop records would
have identical whole-process integrals in both charts by change of variables
on gauge orbits. The only possible difference comes from the declared
finite-dimensional approximations: Jacobian truncation, boundary conventions,
residual root-gauge averaging, or local ansatz truncation. These are exactly
the terms included in `\Theta_{n,k}`. Since the defect vanishes on each finite
battery, Definition 6.1 and Theorem 6.2 give chart independence. `square`

### Theorem 6.7: Elementary-Move Independence

Suppose any two admissible regulator/chart choices can be connected by a
finite chain of elementary moves:

```text
block shape change;
loop approximant change;
gauge-chart/tree change;
smearing-kernel change;
local-counterterm calibration branch change within the same targets.
```

If each elementary move has a vanishing whole-process defect on every finite
battery, then the completed functional is independent of all such choices.

### Proof

Apply Theorem 6.4 or Theorem 6.6 to each elementary move and sum the finitely
many defects. `square`

### Definition 6.8: Certified Whole-Process Independence Ledger

Fix a finite battery `{\mathcal A}_n`. A certified independence ledger between
two regulator/chart declarations `\mathfrak C` and `\mathfrak C'` consists of
a finite chain

```math
\mathfrak C=\mathfrak C^{(0)}
\to
\mathfrak C^{(1)}
\to\cdots\to
\mathfrak C^{(M)}
=\mathfrak C'
```

and nonnegative whole-process comparison errors `\epsilon_{j,n,k}` such that

```math
\sup_{\|F\|_n\le1}
|S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak C^{(j)}}(F)
-S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak C^{(j+1)}}(F)|
\le
\epsilon_{j,n,k}
```

for `0\le j<M`, and

```math
\sum_{j=0}^{M-1}\limsup_{k\to\infty}\epsilon_{j,n,k}=0.
```

The chain may change blocking, chart, loop approximants, smearing kernels, or
counterterm calibration conventions, but every link is a comparison of
complete finite-battery record functionals. The ledger is invalid if any link
requires composing undeclared subprocesses as transition kernels.

### Theorem 6.9: Certified Ledger Gives Regulator/Chart Independence

If every pair of admissible regulator/chart declarations admits a certified
whole-process independence ledger on every finite battery, then the completed
functional is independent of regulator and gauge-chart choices.

### Proof

For `\|F\|_n\le1`, telescope along the certified chain:

```math
|S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak C}(F)-S_{n,k}^{\mathfrak C'}(F)|
\le
\sum_{j=0}^{M-1}\epsilon_{j,n,k}.
```

Taking `\limsup_{k\to\infty}` gives zero by the ledger condition. Hence
Definition 6.1 holds for the finite battery. Theorem 6.2 and the dense
extension theorem then give the same completed functional. `square`

### Definition 6.10: Gauge-Chart Certificate

A gauge-chart transition `\chi\to\chi'` is certified on
`{\mathcal A}_n` if it is represented by:

1. the same declared gauge-orbit integral before finite-dimensional
   truncation;
2. closed-trace scalar loop records, so the tested records are constant on
   gauge orbits;
3. a residual-root-gauge average or boundary convention whose total variation
   defect is bounded by `b_{n,k}`;
4. a Jacobian/Faddeev-Popov truncation defect bounded by `j_{n,k}`;
5. a chart-domain exceptional-set defect bounded by `e_{n,k}`;
6. a local ansatz or finite-mode truncation defect bounded by `t_{n,k}`.

The certificate closes if

```math
b_{n,k}+j_{n,k}+e_{n,k}+t_{n,k}\to0
```

for every fixed finite battery.

### Proposition 6.11: Gauge-Chart Certificate Bounds The Transition Defect

Under Definition 6.10,

```math
\Theta_{n,k}(\chi,\chi')
\le
b_{n,k}+j_{n,k}+e_{n,k}+t_{n,k}.
```

Thus a closing gauge-chart certificate proves the hypothesis of Theorem 6.6.

### Proof

On the common chart domain with the exact Jacobian, closed-trace scalar
Wilson-loop records have identical orbit integrals in both charts. The
remaining discrepancy is exhausted by the declared residual-boundary,
Jacobian, exceptional-set, and finite-mode defects. Taking the supremum over
`\|F\|_n\le1` gives the displayed bound. `square`

## 7. Nonperturbative Anchor Boundary

### Definition 7.1: Nonperturbative Anchor

A nonperturbative anchor is a record `F_*\in{\mathcal A}_{\rm adm}` and a
constant `c_*>0` such that

```math
|S(F_*)-T_{\rm triv}(F_*)|\ge c_*,
```

where `T_{\rm triv}` is the declared trivial Wilson-loop functional.

The anchor is nonperturbative only if the lower bound is obtained without
restricting to a weak-coupling perturbative reference window.

### Theorem 7.2: Nontriviality From A Nonperturbative Anchor

If the completed functional `S` satisfies Definition 7.1, then it is not the
trivial Yang-Mills Wilson-loop functional.

### Proof

Immediate from the displayed inequality. `square`

### Proposition 7.3: Paper 12 Supplies Only A Perturbative Anchor

The Paper-12 anchor is inherited from a small-smearing perturbative
weak-coupling window. Therefore it proves nontriviality of the finite-battery
AF bridge but does not, by itself, prove nonperturbative continuum
Yang-Mills.

### Proof

Paper 12 imports the Paper-11 perturbative smeared anchor and transfers it to
renormalized unsmeared records by small-smearing removal. The lower bound is
valid in the declared weak-coupling reference window. A nonperturbative anchor
would require a lower bound stable across the continuum construction without
using that perturbative restriction. `square`

### Proposition 7.4: Positivity And Covariance Alone Do Not Give An Anchor

Reflection positivity, Euclidean covariance, normalization, and loop
continuity do not imply nontriviality.

### Proof

The trivial functional

```math
T_{\rm triv}(1)=1,
\qquad
T_{\rm triv}(W_\rho(C))=1
```

on normalized scalar Wilson-loop records is normalized, Euclidean invariant,
continuous in the admissible loop topology, and reflection-positive on the
commutative positive-time record algebra. Therefore these structural
properties alone cannot imply a lower bound separating `S` from
`T_{\rm triv}`. A separate anchor is necessary. `square`

### Definition 7.5: Creutz-Type Wilson-Loop Anchor

Let `R,T,\sigma>0` be physical lengths with all rectangles admissible. Define
the perimeter-canceling Creutz-type record

```math
\Xi_{\rho}(R,T;\sigma)
=
S(W_\rho(R,T))S(W_\rho(R-\sigma,T-\sigma))
-
S(W_\rho(R-\sigma,T))S(W_\rho(R,T-\sigma)).
```

It is a nonperturbative Creutz anchor if there exists `c_\Xi>0` such that

```math
|\Xi_{\rho}(R,T;\sigma)|\ge c_\Xi
```

in the completed continuum functional, with the bound obtained without
restricting to a weak-coupling perturbative reference window.

This is an operational Wilson-loop criterion. It does not presuppose a
string, flux tube, particle, or local field ontology.

### Theorem 7.6: Creutz Anchor Implies Nonperturbative Nontriviality

If a Creutz-type Wilson-loop anchor exists, then `S` is nontrivial in the
sense of Definition 7.1.

### Proof

For the trivial normalized Wilson-loop functional, every normalized rectangle
has value `1`, so `\Xi_\rho(R,T;\sigma)=0`. A lower bound
`|\Xi_\rho|\ge c_\Xi>0` separates `S` from `T_{\rm triv}`. `square`

### Definition 7.7: Finite-Volume Running-Coupling Anchor

A finite-volume running-coupling anchor is a family of Wilson-loop records
`G_L\in{\mathcal A}_{\rm adm}` at a physical scale `L` such that:

1. `G_L` is built from admissible renormalized unsmeared Wilson loops;
2. `G_L(T_{\rm triv})=0`;
3. there are constants `0<g_-^2\le g_+^2<\infty` and a physical interval
   `L\in[L_0,L_1]` such that

   ```math
   g_-^2
   \le
   S(G_L)
   \le
   g_+^2
   ```

   without using a weak-coupling perturbative expansion.

### Theorem 7.8: Running-Coupling Anchor Implies Nonperturbative Anchor

If a finite-volume running-coupling anchor exists, then `S` has a
nonperturbative anchor.

### Proof

Since `G_L(T_{\rm triv})=0` and `S(G_L)\ge g_-^2>0`, take
`F_*=G_L` and `c_*=g_-^2` in Definition 7.1. `square`

### Definition 7.9: Nonperturbative Anchor Program

Paper 13 has three honest routes to a nonperturbative anchor:

1. prove a Creutz-type Wilson-loop lower bound as in Definition 7.5;
2. prove a finite-volume running-coupling lower bound as in Definition 7.7;
3. import a constructive nonperturbative Yang-Mills estimate that gives one
   of the above bounds on the declared operational loop algebra.

If none of these routes closes, Paper 13 must not claim full
nonperturbative continuum Yang-Mills. It has only the perturbative-AF
continuum-facing loop functional supplied by Paper 12.

### Definition 7.10: Nonperturbative Anchor Certificate

A nonperturbative anchor certificate for a candidate record `F_*` consists of
finite-volume, finite-refinement, and finite-approximation records
`F_{*,L,k,m}` and constants

```math
c_0>0,
\qquad
v_L,r_k,a_m,q_{L,k,m}\ge0
```

such that:

1. the finite object has a nonperturbative lower bound

   ```math
   |S_{L,k}(F_{*,L,k,m})-T_{\rm triv}(F_{*,L,k,m})|
   \ge c_0;
   ```

2. `v_L` bounds the finite-volume or boundary-condition error;
3. `r_k` bounds the refinement/RG truncation error;
4. `a_m` bounds the loop-approximation and admissible-completion error;
5. `q_{L,k,m}` bounds residual counterterm, chart, and regulator defects;
6. the lower bound is not obtained by restricting to a weak-coupling
   perturbative reference window;
7. along a declared limit path,

   ```math
   v_L+r_k+a_m+q_{L,k,m}\le c_0-c_*
   ```

   for some `c_*>0`.

The four error terms bound the signed-separation error:

```math
\left|
\{S(F_*)-T_{\rm triv}(F_*)\}
-
\{S_{L,k}(F_{*,L,k,m})-T_{\rm triv}(F_{*,L,k,m})\}
\right|
\le
v_L+r_k+a_m+q_{L,k,m}.
```

The certificate is deliberately operational. It is a statement about records
and whole-process errors, not about an assumed underlying field ontology.

### Theorem 7.11: Anchor Certificate Produces A Genuine Nonperturbative Anchor

If a nonperturbative anchor certificate exists, then Definition 7.1 holds
with lower bound `c_*`.

### Proof

By the triangle inequality,

```math
|S(F_*)-T_{\rm triv}(F_*)|
\ge
|S_{L,k}(F_{*,L,k,m})-T_{\rm triv}(F_{*,L,k,m})|
-
\bigl(v_L+r_k+a_m+q_{L,k,m}\bigr).
```

Using the certificate bounds gives

```math
|S(F_*)-T_{\rm triv}(F_*)|
\ge
c_0-(v_L+r_k+a_m+q_{L,k,m})
\ge c_*.
```

Because the lower bound is not derived from a weak-coupling perturbative
window, it is a nonperturbative anchor in the sense of Definition 7.1.
`square`

### Corollary 7.12: Strong-To-Continuum Anchor Transport

Suppose a finite-scale strong-coupling or constructive estimate gives a
Creutz or running-coupling lower bound `c_0>0`, and suppose a whole-process
RG transport ledger carries that record to the continuum admissible algebra
with total error strictly smaller than `c_0`. Then Paper 13 has a genuine
nonperturbative anchor.

### Proof

Use the finite-scale lower bound as item 1 of Definition 7.10 and put the RG
transport, finite-volume, loop-approximation, chart, regulator, and
counterterm losses into `v_L+r_k+a_m+q_{L,k,m}`. Theorem 7.11 applies when
the transported margin remains positive. `square`

### Proposition 7.13: What Would Still Not Be Enough

A formal area-law slogan, a perturbative beta-function calculation, or an
uncalibrated numerical Wilson-loop separation is not yet a Paper-13
nonperturbative anchor. It becomes one only after it is converted into a
certificate of Definition 7.10 on the declared operational loop algebra.

### Proof

Definition 7.10 requires a positive lower bound and a complete error ledger
from the finite object to the completed record. A slogan, perturbative
expansion, or uncalibrated number supplies at most the candidate value, not
the whole-process margin. `square`

### Definition 7.14: Declared Primary Anchor Route

Paper 13 takes the Creutz-type Wilson-loop route as the primary
nonperturbative anchor route.

The reason is ontological, not decorative. A Creutz anchor uses only scalar
closed-loop records and products of their completed values. It therefore stays
inside the operational Wilson-loop algebra and does not presuppose particles,
flux tubes, fields, strings, or a Hilbert-space representation.

The running-coupling route remains an allowed alternative, but it requires an
additional finite-volume coupling definition. The Creutz route is the
shortest Barandes-aligned path because it asks only whether the whole-process
record functional assigns a nontrivial shape-dependent value to a finite
loop battery.

### Definition 7.15: Finite Creutz Anchor Battery

Fix a compact gauge group `G`, a nontrivial unitary representation `\rho`, a
finite physical volume `\Lambda_L`, a whole-process regulator depth `k`, and
a loop approximation depth `m`. Let `R,T,\sigma>0` with
`R-\sigma>0` and `T-\sigma>0`, and let

```math
C_{RT}^{(m)},\quad
C_{R-\sigma,T-\sigma}^{(m)},\quad
```

### Proposition 7.16A: Finite Strong-Coupling Creutz Margin Criterion

Consider a finite lattice heat-kernel gauge measure in volume `\Lambda_L`.
Fix a representation `\rho` and suppose a strong-coupling character/polymer
estimate gives, for each rectangle in the four-loop Creutz battery,

```math
\left|
S_{L,k}(W_\rho(C))-u_\rho^{N(C)}
\right|
\le
\delta_C,
\qquad
0<u_\rho<1,
```

where `N(C)` is the minimal plaquette area of the rectangle and
`u_\rho` is the leading character coefficient. Assume all finite loop values
and leading terms are bounded by `B_\Xi`. Write

```math
N_{RT}+N_{R-\sigma,T-\sigma}
=
N_{R-\sigma,T}+N_{R,T-\sigma}+Q_\sigma,
\qquad
Q_\sigma>0.
```

Let

```math
N_0=N_{R-\sigma,T}+N_{R,T-\sigma}.
```

If

```math
u_\rho^{N_0}(1-u_\rho^{Q_\sigma})
>
B_\Xi(\delta_{RT}+\delta_{R-\sigma,T-\sigma}
+\delta_{R-\sigma,T}+\delta_{R,T-\sigma}),
```

then the finite Creutz battery has a nonperturbative margin with

```math
c_0
=
u_\rho^{N_0}(1-u_\rho^{Q_\sigma})
-
B_\Xi(\delta_{RT}+\delta_{R-\sigma,T-\sigma}
+\delta_{R-\sigma,T}+\delta_{R,T-\sigma}).
```

### Proof

The leading strong-coupling Creutz value is

```math
u_\rho^{N_{RT}+N_{R-\sigma,T-\sigma}}
-
u_\rho^{N_{R-\sigma,T}+N_{R,T-\sigma}}
=
u_\rho^{N_0}(u_\rho^{Q_\sigma}-1),
```

whose absolute value is `u_\rho^{N_0}(1-u_\rho^{Q_\sigma})`. The error in
passing from leading terms to the actual finite Creutz battery is bounded by
the product estimate used in Theorem 7.18, with `e` replaced by the four
strong-coupling remainders `\delta_C`. The displayed strict inequality leaves
the positive margin `c_0`. `square`

This proposition is a finite-regulator statement. It supplies the finite
lower-bound input for Hypothesis 7.16 only when its strong-coupling estimate
is available. It still does not prove continuum Yang-Mills unless the
transport ledger of Definition 7.17 closes with loss smaller than `c_0`.

### Definition 7.17: Creutz Transport Ledger

A Creutz transport ledger consists of nonnegative errors

```math
e_A,\quad e_B,\quad e_C,\quad e_D
```

and a uniform value bound `B_\Xi\ge1` such that the completed continuum
values `A,B,C,D` for the four limiting rectangles obey

```math
|A-A_{L,k,m}|\le e_A,\quad
|B-B_{L,k,m}|\le e_B,\quad
|C-C_{L,k,m}|\le e_C,\quad
|D-D_{L,k,m}|\le e_D,
```

and all finite and limiting loop values have absolute value at most `B_\Xi`.
Each `e` is required to be decomposed into declared whole-process losses:

```text
finite-volume loss;
refinement/RG loss;
loop-approximation loss;
regulator/chart/counterterm loss.
```

The ledger is closing if

```math
B_\Xi(e_A+e_B+e_C+e_D)<c_0.
```

### Theorem 7.18: Finite Creutz Margin Transports To The Continuum

Assume Hypothesis 7.16 and a closing Creutz transport ledger. Then the
completed Creutz value satisfies

```math
|\Xi_\rho(R,T;\sigma)|
\ge
c_0-B_\Xi(e_A+e_B+e_C+e_D).
```

In particular, it is nonzero if the ledger closes.

### Proof

For products,

```math
|AB-A_{L,k,m}B_{L,k,m}|
\le
B_\Xi(e_A+e_B),
```

and similarly

```math
|CD-C_{L,k,m}D_{L,k,m}|
\le
B_\Xi(e_C+e_D).
```

Therefore

```math
|\Xi_\rho(R,T;\sigma)-\Xi_{L,k,m}|
\le
B_\Xi(e_A+e_B+e_C+e_D).
```

The claimed lower bound follows from the reverse triangle inequality.
`square`

Taking `\eta<c_\Xi/[2(B_\Xi+1)]` contradicts Theorem 7.18. Hence one of the

### Definition 7.23: Scale-Resolved Creutz Transport Ledger

Fix a finite Creutz battery at a declared renormalized block scale `s_0`.
Let

```math
(A_s,B_s,C_s,D_s),
\qquad
s=s_0,s_0+1,\ldots,
```

be the four loop values along a whole-process refinement ledger, with
completed values `(A_\infty,B_\infty,C_\infty,D_\infty)`. The ledger is
scale-resolved if, for each `X\in\{A,B,C,D\}`,

```math
|X_{s+1}-X_s|\le \lambda_{X,s},
\qquad
|X_\infty-X_{s_0}|
\le
\Lambda_X,
```

where

```math
\Lambda_X
=
f_X(L)+\ell_X(m)+q_X(\chi)
+\sum_{s=s_0}^{\infty}\lambda_{X,s}.
```

Here:

1. `f_X(L)` is the finite-volume or boundary-condition tail;
2. `\ell_X(m)` is the loop-approximation tail;
3. `q_X(\chi)` is the regulator/chart/counterterm certificate tail;
4. `\lambda_{X,s}` is the declared whole-process RG/refinement loss between
   adjacent scales.

The ledger has reserve `\kappa_{\rm res}\in(0,1)` relative to a finite Creutz margin
`c_0` if all finite and completed loop values are bounded by `B_\Xi\ge1` and

```math
B_\Xi\sum_{X\in\{A,B,C,D\}}\Lambda_X
\le
(1-\kappa_{\rm res})c_0.
```

### Theorem 7.24: Positive-Margin Survival Under A Scale-Resolved Ledger

Assume a finite Creutz margin

```math
|\Xi_{s_0}|\ge c_0>0
```

at the renormalized block scale `s_0`. If the scale-resolved transport ledger
has reserve `\kappa_{\rm res}`, then the completed Creutz value satisfies

```math
|\Xi_\infty|\ge \kappa_{\rm res} c_0.
```

Consequently, at least one scalar Wilson-loop record in the Creutz battery is
separated from the trivial functional by

```math
c_*=\frac{\kappa_{\rm res} c_0}{2(B_\Xi+1)}.
```

### Proof

The scale-resolved ledger gives

```math
|A_\infty-A_{s_0}|\le\Lambda_A,
\quad
|B_\infty-B_{s_0}|\le\Lambda_B,
\quad
|C_\infty-C_{s_0}|\le\Lambda_C,
\quad
|D_\infty-D_{s_0}|\le\Lambda_D.
```

Using the uniform bound `B_\Xi`,

```math
|A_\infty B_\infty-A_{s_0}B_{s_0}|
\le
B_\Xi(\Lambda_A+\Lambda_B),
```

and

```math
|C_\infty D_\infty-C_{s_0}D_{s_0}|
\le
B_\Xi(\Lambda_C+\Lambda_D).
```

Therefore

```math
|\Xi_\infty-\Xi_{s_0}|
\le
B_\Xi\sum_X\Lambda_X
\le
(1-\kappa_{\rm res})c_0.
```

The reverse triangle inequality gives

```math
|\Xi_\infty|
\ge
c_0-(1-\kappa_{\rm res})c_0
=
\kappa_{\rm res} c_0.
```

The scalar record separation follows from the same argument as Corollary
7.19, replacing `c_\Xi` by `\kappa_{\rm res} c_0`. `square`

### Definition 7.25: AF-Compatible Summable Transport Profile

Let the cutoff scale be `a_s=a_{s_0}2^{-(s-s_0)}`. A scale-resolved Creutz
ledger is AF-compatible with exponents `\omega,\nu>0` if the adjacent-scale
losses have the form

```math
\lambda_{X,s}
\le
K_X 2^{-\omega(s-s_0)}(1+\beta_0(s-s_0))^{-p}
+
H_X2^{-\nu(s-s_0)},
```

with `K_X,H_X,\beta_0,p\ge0`, and if `f_X(L)`, `\ell_X(m)`, and
`q_X(\chi)` are separately controllable by finite-volume, loop-approximation,
and certificate choices.

The factor `2^{-\omega(s-s_0)}` is the essential locality/irrelevance gain.
The logarithmic asymptotic-freedom factor alone is not enough to guarantee
summability.

### Theorem 7.26: AF-Compatible Profile Closes The Transport Ledger

Under Definition 7.25,

```math
\sum_{s=s_0}^{\infty}\lambda_{X,s}
\le
\frac{K_X}{1-2^{-\omega}}
+
\frac{H_X}{1-2^{-\nu}}.
```

Thus the Creutz margin survives with reserve `\kappa_{\rm res}` whenever

```math
B_\Xi
\sum_X
\left[
f_X(L)+\ell_X(m)+q_X(\chi)
+\frac{K_X}{1-2^{-\omega}}
+\frac{H_X}{1-2^{-\nu}}
\right]
\le
(1-\kappa_{\rm res})c_0.
```

### Proof

Since `(1+\beta_0(s-s_0))^{-p}\le1`, the first sum is bounded by the
geometric series `K_X/(1-2^{-\omega})`; the second by
`H_X/(1-2^{-\nu})`. The displayed inequality is exactly the reserve
condition in Definition 7.23, so Theorem 7.24 applies. `square`

### Proposition 7.28: Why A Microscopic Strong-Coupling Margin Is Not Enough

Suppose the only available finite margin is a microscopic strong-coupling
margin `c_0(a)` that tends to zero along the cutoff removal, while the
declared transport losses have a nonzero lower envelope or decay more slowly
than `c_0(a)`. Then Paper 13 cannot close the nonperturbative anchor gate
from that margin.

### Proof

`(1-\kappa_{\rm res})c_0(a)`. If `c_0(a)\to0` and the total transport loss does not
vanish faster than `c_0(a)`, no fixed `\kappa_{\rm res}>0` can satisfy the
inequality.
Thus the correct finite anchor must be a renormalized/block-scale margin or a
constructive continuum lower bound, not merely a shrinking microscopic
strong-coupling signal. `square`
