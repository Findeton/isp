# Submission plan — "Non-Markovian Gravitational Decoherence" (paper 1)

For an independent researcher with no institutional affiliation and no contacts. Honest,
staged, and realistic.

## The key reframe

**You do not need contacts or an affiliation to submit to a journal.** Journal submission
portals are open to anyone with an email address. Contacts help with two *optional* things —
(1) getting onto arXiv (which needs an endorser), and (2) informal pre-feedback — but **neither
is required to get peer-reviewed and published**. There are two independent paths, and you can
run them in parallel:

- **Path A — arXiv preprint** (visibility + priority; needs an endorser).
- **Path B — direct journal submission** (the actual publication; needs nothing but the portal).

Path B is the one fully under your control.

## Stage 0 — set expectations honestly

- This is a **well-scoped speculative phenomenology paper**, not a breakthrough. Its strengths
  are (a) a concrete, falsifiable discriminator — now three-axis (onset shape × `E_G`-scaling ×
  a non-Gaussian fingerprint), with `τ_c` pinned to `τ_G` (forced, not fitted) — and (b) unusual
  honesty about what is and isn't derived. Its acceptance risk: it's "motivated by ISP," not a
  derivation, and the onset is shared with the collapse-model family — referees will press on exactly
  that (multiple internal review rounds have hardened it against those objections, and the third axis
  now separates it from generic colored noise).
- **Realistic success = an arXiv/Zenodo preprint that's on the record and citable, plus
  (after 1–3 attempts and revisions) publication in a foundations or quantum-gravity-phenomenology
  journal.** Not PRL, not fame. A genuine, real contribution at the right scale.
- **Timeline:** prep (days) → arXiv attempt (1–4 weeks, may need retries) → journal first
  decision (1–4 months) → revisions → acceptance (another few months). Realistically **6–12
  months** to a published paper if accepted, possibly across 2 journal attempts.
- **Cost:** can be **€0**. arXiv, Zenodo, and *paywalled* (non-open-access) journal publication
  are free. Only optional open-access (APC) costs money — skip it.

## Stage 2 — arXiv (Path A; do in parallel, not blocking)

arXiv is where this subfield lives; a preprint there is read, citable, and establishes priority.

- **The one hurdle: endorsement.** With a non-academic (gmail) address and no prior arXiv papers,
  you need **one endorser** for the category (target: **quant-ph**, cross-list **gr-qc**). An
  endorser is an established author in that category who clicks a link to vouch that your paper is
  *appropriate for the category* — **not** that it's correct (it's not peer review).
- **How to get endorsed:** email *one* active researcher in the exact subfield (someone whose work
  your paper engages — the collapse-model / gravitational-decoherence community) a short, humble
  note + the PDF, explaining you're independent and seeking an arXiv endorsement. Some are
  generous; expect that several won't reply. This doubles as your feedback request (Stage 2b).
- **Fallback if endorsement stalls:** post to **Zenodo** (CERN-backed, free, gives a real,
  permanent, citable **DOI**) or **OSF Preprints**. Legitimate and permanent, just lower
  visibility than arXiv. **Do NOT use viXra** — it's associated with cranks and will hurt you.
  You can move to arXiv later once you have a published paper (which auto-qualifies you).

### Stage 2b — informal feedback (optional but valuable)

- Email 1–3 researchers in the subfield a *specific, short* question — not "review my theory,"
  but e.g. "I find that non-Markovian gravitational decoherence gives a zero-initial-slope
  (quadratic) onset that the strict DP exponential lacks; is this onset-shape discriminator
  already known / does it survive the spontaneous-radiation argument?" Specificity + honesty are
  your assets. One substantive reply is worth a lot (and may yield your endorser).

## Stage 3 — journal submission (Path B; the real goal)

Submit directly through the journal's online portal. No contacts needed.

**Recommended targets, in order:**

1. **Foundations of Physics** (Springer) — best topical fit for a foundations/collapse paper,
   accustomed to interpretation-adjacent and independent-author work. **Free to publish**
   (paywalled); OA optional. *Primary recommendation.*
2. **Quantum** (quantum-journal.org) — reputable, **free**, open-access, community-run; leans
   quantum-information but takes foundations. Good alternative.
3. **AVS Quantum Science** or **New Journal of Physics** — reasonable mid-tier homes.
4. **Physical Review D** (gravitational) or **Physical Review A** (foundations) — the ambitious
   option; free to submit/publish paywalled, but referees are tougher on speculative phenomenology.
   Worth a shot if the first round goes well.

**Avoid:** MDPI *Entropy* and similar (high APC ~€2500, mixed reputation); anything that *emails
you* soliciting a submission (predatory); viXra.

**Cover letter (1 page) should say, plainly:**
- what the paper shows (DP exponential = white-noise limit; finite memory → quadratic onset; a
  fit-robust three-axis discriminator — shape × `E_G`-scaling × non-Gaussian fingerprint — with `τ_c`
  pinned to `τ_G`, forced not fitted);
- that it's a *motivated phenomenological alternative*, explicitly not a first-principles
  derivation (your honesty is a feature — state it);
- why it fits the journal;
- that it's not under consideration elsewhere;
- suggested referees.

## Stage 4 — surviving peer review

- **Expect** "reject" or "major revision" on the first try. That's normal, not failure.
- The internal review rounds (now several) already pre-empt the predictable objections
  (under-derivation, uniqueness, radiation bound, normalization, the `τ_c` closure, environmental
  mimics, the state-dependence/linearity question). Keep that discipline in your replies: concede
  what's fair, defend what's defensible, never overclaim.
- If rejected, take the referee comments, revise, and submit to the next journal on the list. Two
  to three attempts is typical and not a stigma.

## One strategic warning (important)

**Submit this paper strictly standalone.** Do not attach, cite, or allude to the broader
ISP/RISP/Yang–Mills corpus in the submission. The paper already cites only external literature and
frames ISP modestly — keep it exactly that way. Bundling it with the grand program would trigger
"crank" pattern-matching and sink an otherwise credible paper. One clean, narrow, honest paper is
your best ambassador; let it stand on its own.

## Decide your actual goal

- If your goal is **"put the idea on the record, citable and findable"** → arXiv (or Zenodo) alone
  achieves it, free, fast, no gatekeeping. That may be enough.
- If your goal is **a peer-reviewed stamp** → add Path B (journal), accepting the time and
  gatekeeping.

Both are legitimate. Be clear which you want before spending months on review.
