## Abstract (plain text, trimmed to fit arXiv's ~1920-char limit)

Gravitationally induced decoherence, in the Diósi–Penrose (DP) form, predicts that a spatial superposition of a massive body loses coherence with characteristic time tau_G = hbar/E_G, where E_G is the gravitational self-energy of the mass-density difference between the branches. The DP law is exponential in time — a form that follows from an implicit Markov (white-noise, divisible) assumption about the underlying stochastic dynamics, not from gravity itself. Motivated by Barandes' indivisible stochastic processes (ISP), whose defining feature is the failure of divisibility (Chapman–Kolmogorov), we replace that assumption with a finite-memory one (correlation time tau_c). ISP motivates the finite-memory channel; a division-event model grounds the kernel and pins tau_c (ansatz). Any finite, regular noise with no white component then replaces the DP exponential by a non-exponential decay whose short-time exponent is quadratic — Gaussian, C(T) = C(0) exp[-(1/2)(T/tau_G)^2], when the fluctuation scale is of order E_G and tau_c is at least of order tau_G — crossing over to a DP-slope tail at later times; the strict DP exponential is recovered in the white-noise limit (tau_c -> 0 at fixed noise power). The distinction is experimentally sharp and rate-robust: the residual gravitational log-coherence has finite initial slope (linear) for Markovian collapse but zero initial slope (quadratic, a Zeno-like plateau) for the indivisible case, while the scaling of the rate with E_G separates the gravitational channel from non-gravitational collapse models. We give threshold masses and show the radiation/heating bounds constrain only the model's regularization, not the decoherence. The quadratic onset is the generic signature of non-Markovianity, shared with coloured-noise collapse models, so the test falsifies strict Markovian DP/CSL and is evidence for — but does not by itself single out — ISP.
