## Abstract

Paper XXIX found the first genuinely bridge-like finite object after the projection-sufficiency campaign:

$$
\bar H_R(a,b)
=
\mathbb P(
\text{deleted child has score }a
\text{ and score loss }b
\mid
\text{parent record }R
).
$$

Here `R` is a committed unlabeled record order. The number `a` is the hidden score of the deleted child presentation, and `b` is how much hidden score was lost by deleting one point. The object `\bar H_R(a,b)` is not the full hidden presentation. It is the compressed boundary histogram seen from a committed record.

Paper XXIX showed:

1. the raw boundary tensor `\bar B` is exact but too reconstructive;
2. the compressed histogram `\bar H` reconstructs the normalized hidden score polynomial `\bar W`;
3. small finite local covers make `\bar H` constant on record atoms at `N=7` and `N=8`;
4. those covers drift across `N`.

The obvious next idea is: because `\bar H` is a deletion-boundary object, maybe the right local operators are not ordinary unrooted flags, but **rooted boundary flags**.

This paper tests that idea fully in the finite audited window.

## 2. Symbols

Let `N` be the number of committed records.

Let `\pi` be a hidden labeled presentation, represented in the finite receipts by a permutation. The hidden presentation is not treated as physical ontology. It is a proof device used to generate and stress-test committed record laws.

Let

$$
\rho_N(\pi)=R
$$

mean: take the hidden presentation `\pi`, forget the labels, and keep only the committed unlabeled record order `R`.

Let

$$
d_i\pi
$$

be the hidden presentation obtained by deleting the `i`-th point of `\pi` and standardizing the remaining presentation. This is a deletion/decomposition operator, not physical time evolution.

Let

$$
s_N(\pi)
$$

be the finite hidden score used in the adversarial receipts. In the Paper XXIX/Paper XXX receipts this is the same coarse-block score that exposes staged/fiber modulation.

For a deletion of `\pi`, define

$$
a
=
s_{N-1}(d_i\pi),
$$

the child score, and

$$
b
=
s_N(\pi)-s_{N-1}(d_i\pi),
$$

the score loss caused by deleting that point.

The raw rooted boundary tensor is

$$
\bar B_N^{\downarrow}(R,A;a,b),
$$

where:

- `R` is the parent committed record;
- `A` is the child committed record after deletion;
- `a` is the child score;
- `b` is the score loss.

This tensor is exact, but too close to reconstructing hidden presentations. It is therefore not a plausible final click-law object.

The compressed boundary histogram is

$$
\bar H_R(a,b)
=
\mathbb P(
s_{N-1}(d_i\pi)=a,\,
s_N(\pi)-s_{N-1}(d_i\pi)=b
\mid
\rho_N(\pi)=R
).
$$

This forgets the child record `A` and keeps only the boundary score profile. It is still record-projected because it is conditioned only on the parent committed record `R`.

The normalized hidden score polynomial is

$$
\bar W_R(u)
=
\mathbb E(
u^{s_N(\pi)}
\mid
\rho_N(\pi)=R
).
$$

The bridge identity is:

$$
\bar W_R(u)
=
\sum_{a,b}
\bar H_R(a,b)\,u^{a+b}.
$$

So `\bar H` is stronger than `\bar W`, but weaker than `\bar B`.

## 3. What a rooted flag is

An ordinary local flag counts small induced suborders inside a record. For example, a `4`-flag counts how often a particular four-record pattern appears.

A rooted flag does the same thing with one point distinguished.

For a committed record `R`:

1. choose a root point `v`;
2. choose `k-1` other points;
3. look at the induced `k`-point order;
4. remember the root position inside that small order;
5. aggregate over all possible roots.

The important point is that the root is not a hidden trajectory. It is summed over. A rooted flag is still a committed record observable.

This makes it compatible with the Barandes/Shard indivisibility discipline:

> the record is indivisible as ontology; deletion/rooting is a decomposition identity used by the law, not a claim that the event physically splits into smaller events.

## 7. Hostile review

### Objection 1: this just renames Paper XXIX

No. Paper XXIX found `\bar H` and showed it was locally coverable but drifting. Paper XXX tests the natural boundary-language hypothesis: rooted flags should be the correct operators because `\bar H` is a deletion-rooted object.

That hypothesis partly survives and partly fails.

It survives semantically:

> rooted flags are record-intrinsic and deletion-natural.

It fails as a simplification:

> rooted flags do not reduce cover size and do not eliminate drift.

### Objection 2: if rooted and unrooted partitions are identical, rooted flags add nothing

They add semantic discipline, not finite separating power in this window.

The important lesson is that the law should not be written as a list of operator names. If two different operator languages produce the same partition, the partition is more invariant than the coordinates used to describe it.

### Objection 3: the boundary histogram still uses a hidden score

Correct. `\bar H` is not yet the click record law.

Its role is diagnostic:

> it tells us how a hidden projected likelihood leaks into committed records through deletion/insertion boundaries.

The final law needs a record-intrinsic version of the same mechanism, or an admissibility theorem saying exactly when such hidden scores are allowed to be integrated out.

### Objection 4: the raw boundary tensor is exact, so why not use it?

Because exactness is not enough. The raw tensor `\bar B` remembers the child record `A` and is too reconstructive. It risks turning hidden presentations into physical structure.

The compressed histogram `\bar H` is a better bridge because it keeps the boundary response and discards the explicit child identity.

### Objection 5: this is still finite

Yes. The paper proves finite audited facts at `N=7` and `N=8`.

The all-`N` theorem is still open. But the finite campaign was strong enough to falsify three plausible wrong routes:

1. fixed unrooted cover names;
2. fixed rooted cover names;

## 8. The new theorem target

Let `\mathcal X_N` be the committed record space at size `N`.

Let

$$
\mathcal P_N
$$

be a partition of `\mathcal X_N` induced by record-intrinsic local operators.

The boundary histogram is measurable with respect to this partition if:

$$
R_1 \sim_{\mathcal P_N} R_2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\bar H_{R_1}=\bar H_{R_2}.
$$

The audited covers prove this at finite `N` for small selected partitions.

But fixed operator names drift. So the stable theorem should be formulated at the partition level:

> **Rooted-boundary projectivity target.** There exists a record-intrinsic family of partitions `\mathcal P_N`, generated by controlled local rooted-boundary operators, such that:
>
> 1. `\bar H_N` is measurable with respect to `\mathcal P_N`;
> 2. deletion sends `\mathcal P_N` to `\mathcal P_{N-1}` up to controlled refinement/coarsening;
> 3. insertion sends `\mathcal P_{N-1}` back to `\mathcal P_N` up to controlled refinement/coarsening;
> 4. the residual boundary-conflict mass is summable or tends to zero in the physical scaling window;
> 5. the construction is record-covariant: it depends only on committed records, not on hidden labels or hidden trajectories.

This is the precise form of the surviving idea.

It says the click law should be a stable boundary-filtration law, not a finite lookup table and not a hidden process.
