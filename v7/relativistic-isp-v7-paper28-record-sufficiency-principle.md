## 2. The principle

Let `Omega_N` be a hidden construction space, and let

$$
\pi_N:\Omega_N\to\mathcal O_N
$$

forget coordinates, hidden labels, tapes, machines, and uncommitted marks, leaving only the committed finite record order `O_N`.

Let `P_N` be the candidate null law on `O_N`, and let

$$
Q_N=(\pi_N)_\#\widetilde Q_N
$$

be the record pushforward of a hidden refinement `\widetilde Q_N`.

The record sufficiency principle says:

> Hidden structure is admissible only through `Q_N`. If `Q_N` is record-contiguous with `P_N`, it is not an additional record ontology. If `Q_N` is not record-contiguous with `P_N`, the diverging likelihood residue is a missing record mark, sector, or action.

One convenient sufficient test is the second moment:

$$
S_N
=
\mathbb E_{P_N}
\left[
\left(
\frac{dQ_N}{dP_N}
\right)^2
\right]
=
1+\chi^2(Q_N\|P_N).
$$

It controls record-observable total variation:

$$
\operatorname{TV}(Q_N,P_N)
\le
\frac12
\sqrt{S_N-1}.
$$

For asymptotic contiguity one wants stronger control than one finite total-variation bound, but the message is the same: the Radon-Nikodym derivative on the record sigma-field is the right object.

## 3. Why this is not just maximum entropy

Maximum entropy is tempting but incomplete. It says "choose the least structured law under constraints." The previous papers show that the problem is not only choosing a law; it is deciding what counts as a legitimate hidden refinement.

Two hidden mechanisms can share coarse entropy and scalar features while differing in record-visible likelihood. Conversely, a deterministic hidden machine can realize exactly the same record law as a stochastic clock. Entropy alone does not decide this.

Record sufficiency does:

$$
\text{same record pushforward}
\Rightarrow
\text{same record physics}.
$$

and

$$
\text{different non-contiguous record pushforward}
\Rightarrow
\text{missing record variable/action}.
$$

Maximum entropy may be a tool inside `P_N`. It is not the principle that decides hidden admissibility.

## 4. Why this is not just an action

Quadratic/bracket actions were a real advance. They prevent signed cancellation and catch many staged orders. But they are still projections. A finite action can miss a tuned law if the tuned law remains close in the chosen observable basis.

Record sufficiency says what the action is trying to approximate:

$$
\log \frac{dQ_N}{dP_N}
$$

on the committed record sigma-field.

In the Gaussian/quadratic regime, a bracket action is the second-order approximation to this log-likelihood. Outside that regime, the full likelihood ratio is the real object.

This explains why Paper XXVII ended at the second moment, not at another quadratic score.

## 8. Finite sufficiency theorem

The third receipt, `p28_formal_sufficiency_theorem.py`, proves the finite theorem behind the principle. Let `P` and `Q` be two laws on a finite committed record space, and let

$$
L=\frac{dQ}{dP},
\qquad
S=\mathbb E_P[L^2].
$$

Then every record event `A` obeys

$$
Q(A)
=
\mathbb E_P[L\mathbf 1_A]
\le
\sqrt{S}\sqrt{P(A)}.
$$

Therefore, if a family has

$$
\sup_N S_N<\infty,
$$

then every sequence of record events with `P_N(A_N)\to 0` also has `Q_N(A_N)\to 0`. This is the finite algebraic core of contiguity.

The same receipt checks the total-variation corollary:

$$
\operatorname{TV}(Q,P)
\le
\frac12\sqrt{S-1}.
$$

It also checks the data-processing rule:

$$
S(\phi_\#Q\|\phi_\#P)\le S(Q\|P)
$$

for every coarse record feature `\phi`. This is why finite interval profiles, finite bracket fields, and finite flag counts are useful but not final. A coarse feature failure is decisive, because full divergence must be at least as large. A coarse feature pass is not decisive, because coarsening can erase the likelihood ratio.

Finally, independent repetition multiplies the second moment:

$$
S(P^{\otimes m},Q^{\otimes m})=S(P,Q)^m.
$$

So even small nontrivial residue becomes visible if it is repeatedly sampled as a real record sector. Conversely, bounded second moment is exactly the statement that no rare record class is carrying unbounded hidden predictive residue.

This is the first hard gain of Paper XXVIII:

> The principle is not only philosophical. On any finite record space, the second moment is the exact obstruction to hiding predictive residue from committed records.

This is the second hard gain of Paper XXVIII:

## 14. Canonical-kernel contraction lemma

The ninth receipt, `p28_canonical_kernel_contraction_lemma.py`, attacks the structural reason for the coefficient envelope.

The rank-copula residue is a canonical kernel:

$$

\qquad
\sum_i D_{ij}=0.
$$

Therefore, if endpoints were sampled independently with replacement, every nonempty product moment would vanish. The only terms that survive in the without-replacement expansion are endpoint-collision diagrams. A collision diagram identifies some of the `2r` endpoints of

$$
\prod_{k=1}^r D_{a_k b_k}.
$$

After identification, it becomes a quotient multigraph whose edges are the original kernel factors. Row-zero degeneracy kills every quotient graph with a leaf: summing over the leaf vertex gives a zero row sum. Loops also vanish because the diagonal is absent. Thus only 2-core quotient graphs survive.

The receipt enumerates all endpoint partitions for `r<=5` and verifies the pattern:

|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|

| `2` | `15` | `7` | `2` | `2` | `2` |
| `3` | `203` | `87` | `12` | `3` | `3` |
| `4` | `4140` | `1657` | `212` | `4` | `4` |
| `5` | `115975` | `43833` | `5120` | `5` | `5` |

This explains the exponent:

> a surviving order-`r` canonical-kernel contraction has at most `r` quotient vertices, hence at least `r` endpoint identifications, hence an `N^{-r}` sampling penalty.

The missing analytic lemma is now narrower. It is not the exponent; row-zero degeneracy gives that. It is the activity bound on the surviving 2-core contractions. In schematic form, if the absolute 2-core activity at order `r` is bounded by

The conclusion is precise:

> Row-zero degeneracy gives the `N^{-r}` exponent. The remaining theorem is a uniform exponential bound on the absolute activities of the surviving 2-core contractions for the physical rank-copula.

## 22. Interval-to-subset bridge campaign

\sum_{I\in\mathcal I}
w_I
\frac{\mathbf 1_{\{S\subset I\}}}{\binom{|I|}{2r}}

No. It kills the naive coverage claim. Proper intervals can miss `2r`-subsets completely, so interval averages do not automatically imply all-subset control. Recursive heredity remains useful only if the interval family is accompanied by an exact/dominating design theorem, or if the missing subset certificates are promoted as an explicit record sector.

### Review 30: "Just include the full interval."

That is the tautological escape. A domination theorem can assign smaller full-set mass, but then it pays `C>=1/epsilon`. That may be admissible as a charged global sector; it is not a free local interval theorem.

### Review 31: "The uncovered mass is only `O(N^-2)`, so maybe it is harmless."

False for the all-order coefficient-root target. The endpoint-spanning witness has `rho=O(N^-2)`, but `q_{N,r}=binom(N/2,r)rho^2`, so `Nq_{N,r}^{1/r}` grows like `N^{2-4/r}` for every `r>2`. The missed mass is harmless only at `r=2`; the click-law envelope needs all orders.

False. The physical kernel itself violates the absolute missed-residue envelope in the audited grid. The signed residue passes, but the absolute residue reaches `1.70452030128398689` in beta units at `N=14,c=0.5`. The cancellation is genuinely signed.

Only the leading with-replacement term. Row-zero makes endpoint-interior and interior-interior expectation factors vanish, but the actual coefficient is a without-replacement finite-population correction. Balanced-block and Fourier adversaries show that correction can still be too large.

### Review 34: "Then prove missed-residue cancellation as the missing bridge."

Not as an all-order independent bridge. At `r=N/2`, the missed subset is the whole record set, so `q_miss=q_global` for every kernel. An all-order missed-residue theorem contains the original coefficient theorem. It is a diagnostic, not an escape hatch.

### Review 35: "The final signed-envelope campaign still did not prove the physical theorem."

It proves that row-zero, exact signed expansion, `q_2` calibration, smooth Fourier origin, and crude spectral participation are not enough. ### Review 36: "Maybe the theorem should hold for all widths."
