## Abstract

V4 Paper 9 shows that the unconditioned residual minimum is the wrong
GR-facing target.  It can vanish for a flat or kinematic witness without
proving nontrivial dynamical geometry.  Paper 10 therefore studies the
source-conditioned minimum

$$
a_a^*({\mathcal B})
:=
\min_{q\in{\mathcal B}_a}{\mathcal A}_a(q),
$$

where \({\mathcal B}_a\) fixes ordinary finite metric, source, orientation,
boundary, and comparison records.

The main result is a precise side theorem.

First, if the conditioned sector is generated by a smooth compatible finite
deformation atlas, then residual-small witnesses exist:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}SRC\text{-}COND\text{-}SMOOTH\text{-}REALIZABILITY}^{decl}.
}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
a_a^*({\mathcal B})\to0.
$$

Second, if a finite dual separator exists on the conditioned sector, then the
sector has a positive residual floor:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}DUAL\text{-}FLOOR\text{-}CERTIFICATE}.
}
$$

Third, Paper 10 identifies the hidden third case.  Without a finite
regularity and projective compactness collar, the residuals may tend to zero
through singular drifting records that have no smooth GR-facing limit.  This
is not a floor and not a useful positive theorem.

The cure is also precise.  On closed, projectively compact, regular
source-conditioned sectors, the side decision is exact:

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}REGULAR\text{-}COMPACT\text{-}SIDE\text{-}DECISION}.
}
$$

For such sectors,

$$
a_a^*({\mathcal B})\to0
$$

if and only if the projective limit contains a residual-zero regular
deformation atlas.  If no such limit exists, then

$$
\liminf_a a_a^*({\mathcal B})>0.
$$

This is a real narrowing of the V4 attack surface.  What remains is no
longer the abstract residual-minimum problem.  It is the actual-law
identification problem: prove that the finite matter-geometry law supplies
the regular source-conditioned sector and smooth witness family, or produce a
dual floor certificate for the sector it actually supplies.

## 1. The Source-Conditioned Data Packet

### Definition 1.1: Finite Source-Conditioned Data Packet

A finite source-conditioned data packet is a family

$$
{\mathfrak D}
=
({\mathfrak D}_a)_{a\downarrow0}
$$

where each \({\mathfrak D}_a\) specifies:

1. a finite cell complex \(\Lambda_a\);
2. metric readout records \(m_{ij,a}(x)\);
3. orientation-quorum records \(o_{ij,a}(x)\);
4. source or matter records \(s_a(x)\);
5. boundary collar records \(b_a\);
6. a finite test battery of vector fields and lapses
   \({\mathcal T}_{DD,a},{\mathcal T}_{DH,a},{\mathcal T}_{HH,a}\);
7. admissible comparison stencils \(D_a[v]\) and \(H_a[N]\);
8. projective prolongation maps \(P_{a\to b}\) for \(b<a\);
9. tolerances \(\eta_a\to0\).

All entries are finite labels, finite tables, or finite tolerances.

### Definition 1.2: Conditioned Sector Generated By A Packet

The sector \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\subset C_a^{tot}\) consists of
all total finite records \(q=(m,g)\) such that:

1. their metric readouts agree with \(m_{ij,a}\) to tolerance \(\eta_a\);
2. their orientation records agree with \(o_{ij,a}\) to tolerance \(\eta_a\);
3. their source records agree with \(s_a\) to tolerance \(\eta_a\);
4. their boundary collar agrees with \(b_a\);
5. their comparison stencils lie in the admissible stencil family;
6. their prolongations to finer regulators respect the packet to
   \(o(1)\).

The conditioned residual minimum is

$$
a_a^*({\mathfrak D})
:=
\min_{q\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})}{\mathcal A}_a(q).
$$

### Proposition 1.3: The Packet Is Barandes-Aligned

The sector \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) is Barandes-aligned.

Proof.

It is a subset of a finite configuration space defined by finite record
conditions.  It does not introduce a hidden state, a Hilbert phase, or a
continuum equation.  The source and boundary data are ordinary labels in the
finite record law.  `square`

### Warning 1.4: Overconditioning And Underconditioning

The packet can fail in two opposite ways.

1. It can overcondition: the records may be mutually inconsistent, so no
   smooth or regular finite witness exists.
2. It can undercondition: the packet may allow flat or trivial sectors, so
   \(a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\to0\) proves too little.

Paper 10 therefore needs both realizability and regularity.

## 2. Smooth Compatible Packets

### Definition 2.1: Smooth Compatible Packet

A data packet \({\mathfrak D}\) is smoothly compatible if there exist:

1. a compact smooth spatial patch \(\Omega\);
2. a smooth positive definite metric \(h\) on \(\Omega\);
3. smooth source fields \(\sigma\) for the finite source records;
4. smooth boundary data \(\beta_\partial\);
5. smooth vector-field and lapse representatives of the test battery;
6. finite quantization maps \(Q_a\);
7. finite comparison maps generated by discretized whole-slab transports of
   the smooth deformation groupoid;

such that the finite packet records are the \(Q_a\)-quantizations of this
smooth data up to a total tolerance

$$
\epsilon_a^{src}
:=
\epsilon_a^{met}
+\epsilon_a^{ori}
+\epsilon_a^{matter}
+\epsilon_a^{bdry}
+\epsilon_a^{cmp}
+\epsilon_a^{proj}
+\epsilon_a^{obs}
\to0.
$$

The smooth data are not primitive ontology.  They are a certificate that the
finite packet is a coherent approximation family.

### Definition 2.2: Smooth Witness Configuration

Given a smoothly compatible packet, the smooth witness configuration

$$
q_a[h,\sigma]\in C_a^{tot}
$$

is the finite total record obtained by quantizing:

1. \(h\);
2. \(\sigma\);
3. the boundary collar;
4. the orientation-quorum signs;
5. the whole-slab deformation comparison maps.

The witness is accepted only if

$$
q_a[h,\sigma]\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})
$$

for all sufficiently fine \(a\).

### Lemma 2.3: Smooth Witness Belongs To The Conditioned Sector

If \({\mathfrak D}\) is smoothly compatible and its tolerances satisfy
\(\eta_a\ge C\epsilon_a^{src}\), then

$$
q_a[h,\sigma]\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})
$$

for all sufficiently fine \(a\).

Proof.

Each defining finite record of \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) is, by
smooth compatibility, the quantization of the same smooth data used to build
\(q_a[h,\sigma]\), up to error at most \(C\epsilon_a^{src}\).  The tolerance
condition absorbs that error.  `square`

### Theorem 2.4: Source-Conditioned Smooth Realizability

If \({\mathfrak D}\) is smoothly compatible and the finite comparison maps
approximate the smooth hypersurface deformation groupoid on the Paper-7 test
battery, then

$$
a_a^*({\mathfrak D})
\le
C_{\mathfrak D}(\epsilon_a^{src})^2.
$$

In particular,

$$
a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\to0.
$$

This proves

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}SRC\text{-}COND\text{-}SMOOTH\text{-}REALIZABILITY}^{decl}.
}
$$

Proof.

By Lemma 2.3 the witness \(q_a[h,\sigma]\) lies in the conditioned sector.
By smooth compatibility the finite comparison maps approximate the smooth
deformation groupoid, and the smooth groupoid has the standard kinematic
closure identities:

$$
[D[v],D[w]]=D[[v,w]],
$$

$$
[D[v],H[N]]=H[v(N)],
$$

and

$$
[H[N],H[M]]
=
D[h^{ij}(N\partial_jM-M\partial_jN)].
$$

The finite residuals are precisely the finite differences between the left
and right sides of these comparison identities, evaluated on the finite test
battery and finite observable class.  The total comparison, observation,
boundary, source, and projective errors are bounded by
\(\epsilon_a^{src}\).  Hence each residual norm is bounded by
\(C_{\mathfrak D}\epsilon_a^{src}\).  Squaring and summing over the finite
test battery gives

$$
{\mathcal A}_a(q_a[h,\sigma])
\le
C_{\mathfrak D}(\epsilon_a^{src})^2.
$$

Since \(a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\) is the minimum over the conditioned sector, it
is no larger than the witness value.  `square`

## 3. Dual Floor Certificates

### Definition 3.1: Conditioned Residual Vector

Let

$$
{\mathcal R}_a(q)
:=
\bigl(R_{DD,a}^q,R_{DH,a}^q,R_{HH,a}^q,{\mathcal E}_{cov,a}^q\bigr)
$$

in a finite normed residual space \(V_a\).  Then

$$
{\mathcal A}_a(q)=\|{\mathcal R}_a(q)\|^2.
$$

### Definition 3.2: Source-Conditioned Dual Floor Certificate

A source-conditioned dual floor certificate for \({\mathfrak D}\) is a
sequence \(\Lambda_a\in V_a^*\) and a number \(\delta>0\) such that for all
sufficiently fine \(a\):

$$
\|\Lambda_a\|_*\le1,
$$

and

$$
\Lambda_a({\mathcal R}_a(q))\ge\delta
\qquad
\hbox{for all }q\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D}).
$$

### Theorem 3.3: Dual Certificate Gives Conditioned Floor

If a source-conditioned dual floor certificate exists, then

$$
\liminf_a a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\ge\delta^2.
$$

Proof.

For all \(q\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\),

$$
\delta
\le
\Lambda_a({\mathcal R}_a(q))
\le
\|\Lambda_a\|_*\|{\mathcal R}_a(q)\|
\le
\|{\mathcal R}_a(q)\|.
$$

Thus \({\mathcal A}_a(q)=\|{\mathcal R}_a(q)\|^2\ge\delta^2\).  Minimizing
over \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) gives the result.  `square`

### Proposition 3.4: Smooth Compatibility Blocks Dual Floors

If \({\mathfrak D}\) is smoothly compatible, then no dual floor certificate
with fixed \(\delta>0\) can exist.

Proof.

Theorem 2.4 gives \(q_a\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) with
\(\|{\mathcal R}_a(q_a)\|\to0\).  If a dual floor certificate existed, it
would imply \(\|{\mathcal R}_a(q_a)\|\ge\delta\) for all sufficiently fine
\(a\), a contradiction.  `square`

### Verdict 3.5

The positive and negative routes are mutually exclusive on a fixed
conditioned packet:

$$
\mathrm{smooth\ compatibility}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\hbox{zero side},
$$

whereas

$$
\mathrm{dual\ certificate}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\hbox{floor side}.
$$

What remains is to decide whether every relevant packet is either smoothly
compatible or dual-separated.  That requires regularity and compactness.

## 4. The Hidden Third Case: Singular Drift

### Definition 4.1: Singular Drifting Sequence

A singular drifting residual sequence is a sequence

$$
q_a\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})
$$

such that

$$
{\mathcal A}_a(q_a)\to0,
$$

but no subsequence has a projective regular limit interpretable as a smooth
or weak finite deformation atlas.

### Proposition 4.2: Singular Drift Is Not Ruled Out By Finite Residual
Smallness Alone

Finite residual smallness alone does not rule out singular drift.

Proof.

The residual penalty tests the finite comparison identities on the selected
test battery.  Without finite regularity, tightness, overlap control, and
projective compactness, a sequence can keep those tested residuals small
while moving its geometry labels, source labels, or comparison stencils
through increasingly oscillatory or nonconvergent sectors.  Such a sequence
has no certified smooth limit.  Therefore residual smallness alone is not a
GR-facing positive theorem.  `square`

### Meaning 4.3

The side decision has three apparent possibilities before regularity is
imposed:

1. smooth residual-small realization;
2. positive residual floor;
3. singular residual-small drift.

Only the first is a positive ISP-GR route.  The third is a warning that the
conditioning is too weak.

## 5. Regular Source-Conditioned Sectors

### Definition 5.1: Finite Regularity Collar

A source-conditioned packet has a finite regularity collar of order \(k\) if
the sector \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) imposes finite record bounds
corresponding to:

1. uniform positive-definiteness of metric records;
2. uniform upper metric bounds;
3. finite-difference bounds through order \(k\);
4. overlap consistency for local coordinate records;
5. bounded source records and finite differences through order \(k-1\);
6. bounded comparison-stencil support radius;
7. projective prolongation tightness;
8. boundary collar compatibility.

All bounds are finite record constraints at each regulator.

### Definition 5.2: Closed Projective Compactness

A regular packet is closed projectively compact if every sequence

$$
q_{a_n}\in{\mathcal B}_{a_n}({\mathfrak D}),
\qquad
a_n\downarrow0,
$$

has a projectively convergent subsequence whose limit lies in a closed limit
space

$$
{\mathcal B}_\infty({\mathfrak D}).
$$

The limit space is a space of regular metric/source/comparison records, not a
Hilbert space.

### Definition 5.3: Residual Continuity And Approximability

The residual maps are projectively continuous and approximable if:

1. whenever

$$
q_{a_n}\to q_\infty
$$

and

$$
{\mathcal A}_{a_n}(q_{a_n})\to0
$$

the limiting residual vector vanishes:

$$
{\mathcal R}_\infty(q_\infty)=0.
$$

2. whenever \(q_\infty\in{\mathcal B}_\infty({\mathfrak D})\) satisfies

   $$
   {\mathcal R}_\infty(q_\infty)=0,
   $$

   there are finite representatives
   \(q_a\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) with

   $$
   q_a\to q_\infty,
   \qquad
   {\mathcal A}_a(q_a)\to0.
   $$

### Lemma 5.4: Regularity Removes Singular Drift

In a closed projectively compact regular sector with residual continuity,
there are no singular drifting residual-small sequences.

Proof.

Let \(q_{a_n}\) be residual-small.  Closed projective compactness gives a
convergent subsequence \(q_{a_{n_j}}\to q_\infty\).  Residual continuity
gives \({\mathcal R}_\infty(q_\infty)=0\).  Since the limit lies in the
regular limit space, the sequence has a regular projective residual-zero
limit.  This is not singular drift.  `square`

## 6. Compact Side Decision

### Definition 6.1: Residual-Zero Limit Atlas

A residual-zero limit atlas for \({\mathfrak D}\) is an element

$$

### Theorem 6.2: Regular Compact Side Decision

Assume \({\mathfrak D}\) has:

1. a finite regularity collar;
2. closed projective compactness;
3. residual continuity and approximability;
4. projective residual-minimum compatibility.

Then exactly one of the following holds:

1. \({\mathcal B}_\infty({\mathfrak D})\) contains a residual-zero limit
   atlas, and

   $$
   a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\to0;
   $$

2. \({\mathcal B}_\infty({\mathfrak D})\) contains no residual-zero limit
   atlas, and

   $$
   \liminf_a a_a^*({\mathfrak D})>0.
   $$

This proves

$$
\boxed{
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}REGULAR\text{-}COMPACT\text{-}SIDE\text{-}DECISION}.
}
$$

Proof.

Assume first that a residual-zero limit atlas exists.  By the definition of
residual approximability, it is represented by finite records
\(q_a\in{\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\) whose residuals tend to zero in the
declared topology.  Hence

$$
a_a^*({\mathfrak D})
\le
{\mathcal A}_a(q_a)
\to0.
$$

Conversely, assume there is no residual-zero limit atlas.  Suppose for
contradiction that no positive floor exists.  Then there is a sequence
\(a_n\downarrow0\) and configurations
\(q_{a_n}\in{\mathcal B}_{a_n}({\mathfrak D})\) with

$$
{\mathcal A}_{a_n}(q_{a_n})\to0.
$$

Closed projective compactness gives a convergent subsequence with limit
\(q_\infty\in{\mathcal B}_\infty({\mathfrak D})\).  Residual continuity gives
\({\mathcal R}_\infty(q_\infty)=0\), contradicting the assumption that no
residual-zero limit atlas exists.  Therefore a positive floor exists.
Projective residual-minimum compatibility removes oscillatory loopholes as
in Paper 8.  `square`

### Corollary 6.3: Smooth Side Decision Under Smooth Compactness

If the regularity collar upgrades projective limits to smooth compatible
finite deformation atlases, then exactly one of the following holds:

$$
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}SRC\text{-}COND\text{-}SMOOTH\text{-}REALIZABILITY}

or

$$
\mathrm{V4P10\text{-}DUAL\text{-}FLOOR\text{-}OBSTRUCTION}.
$$

Proof.

The zero-side limit atlas of Theorem 6.2 is smooth by the compactness
upgrade.  If no such smooth atlas exists, Theorem 6.2 gives a positive floor.
Finite-dimensional separation on each regulator, together with projective
stability, gives the dual certificate formulation.  `square`

## 9. Failure Modes And Their Interpretation

### Failure Mode 9.1: Inconsistent Packet

If the metric, orientation, source, and boundary records cannot be realized
by any regular gluable finite jet field, then \({\mathcal B}_a({\mathfrak D})\)
is empty for fine \(a\) or has a detected positive floor.

This is not a failure of ISP-GR.  It means the requested finite data packet
is not an admissible geometry packet.

### Failure Mode 9.2: Underconditioned Packet

If the packet allows flat witnesses while claiming to test nontrivial
geometry, then \(a_a^*({\mathfrak D})\to0\) is weak evidence.  The packet must
be refined by adding finite source, boundary, or curvature records that
force the intended nontrivial sector.

### Failure Mode 9.3: Singular Drift

If residuals become small without compactness, the packet is too weak.  Add a
finite regularity collar or accept that the zero side has no GR-facing
interpretation.

### Failure Mode 9.4: Actual-Law Gap

If smooth witnesses exist but the actual law does not concentrate on them,
the problem is no longer residual-minimum geometry.  It is actual-law
selection.
