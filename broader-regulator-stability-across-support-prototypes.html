Broader Regulator Stability Across Support Prototypes
Raw rule nonuniqueness, normalized strip-moment universality, and the correct stability criterion

Felix Robles Elvira

Draft preprint

March 2026

Paper 6 in the relativistic ISP sequence; exact leading benchmark plus normalized strip-moment stability criterion

Abstract. Phase 3 established the strongest exact leading-order universality statement currently justified: for support pairs whose first visible exchange order is generated by the leading coefficients, the admissible $\lambda$-family shares the same onset-renormalized bond-centered tangential operator. This paper asks what a broader regulator-stability theorem should actually mean once support prototypes and higher coefficients enter.

The first result is exact and negative: raw prototype-level regulator universality already fails at leading order, because the full first coefficient obeys $$ A_{R,\lambda}^{(1)}=\lambda(2-\lambda)A_R^{(1)} $$ for every finite interval support $R$, so different admissible regulators do not share the same microscopic coefficient data. The second result is the correct positive criterion. Under regulator-wise strip closure and the equality of normalized first strip moments across a prototype class, the renormalized smeared exchange commutator has the same bond-centered first-order tangential continuum action for every regulator in the class. The universal object is therefore not a raw local matrix coefficient but a normalized strip-moment package of the transition-law algebra itself.

I. Introduction

The free-model sequel and phase 3 already fixed the conceptual fork. Raw rule uniqueness fails, but restricted onset-renormalized universality survives at the leading visible order. That means any broader stability theorem has to be phrased with great care. It cannot mean literal equality of microscopic coefficients across admissible regulators. It has to mean that, after the right onset normalization and the right bond-centered continuum identification, the surviving exchange operator is the same.

The remaining question is how to formulate that principle once one leaves the leading support-order sector. The answer cannot be guessed from rhetoric about “same continuum physics.” It has to be read from the exact localized transition-law algebra. Phase 4 identified the right higher-order invariant: strip closure with finite first moments. The present paper uses that lesson to state the correct broader regulator-stability theorem.

Main results (informal).
  1. Exact negative constraint. Raw prototype-level regulator universality already fails at leading order across interval supports, because the admissible $\lambda$-family has distinct microscopic first coefficients.
  2. Exact leading benchmark. The phase-3 $\lambda$-family result is the first nontrivial support-prototype stability test: after onset normalization, the leading bond-centered tangential operator is the same.
  3. Conditional broader criterion. If regulator-dependent higher-order exchange commutators close on a common strip-channel family and their normalized first strip moments agree, then the renormalized continuum operator is regulator-independent across the prototype class.

Scope note. This paper gives an exact negative result, an exact leading-order benchmark, and the general regulator-stability theorem in conditional strip-moment form. It does not yet provide explicit higher-order moment computations beyond the exact leading $\lambda$-family case.

II. Exact leading constraints on any regulator-stability claim

Proposition 1 (Exact failure of raw prototype-level regulator universality). For every finite interval support $R$ and every $0<\lambda\le 1$, the admissible localized deformation family satisfies $$ A_{R,\lambda}^{(1)}=c_\lambda A_R^{(1)}, \qquad c_\lambda:=\lambda(2-\lambda). $$ Since $c_\lambda$ varies with $\lambda$, no regulator-independent raw leading-coefficient theorem can hold across the admissible $\lambda$-family for interval prototypes with $A_R^{(1)}\neq 0$.
Proof. The exact free-model sequel proves the operator identity $$ A_{R,\lambda}^{(1)}=c_\lambda A_R^{(1)} $$ for every finite interval support $R$. The factor $c_\lambda=\lambda(2-\lambda)$ is nonconstant on $(0,1]$, so distinct admissible regulators in the family have distinct raw first coefficients whenever $A_R^{(1)}$ is nonzero. $ \square $
Remark. This sharpens the no-go lesson. It is not merely that one special singleton coefficient depends on the regulator. The dependence already propagates through the full leading finite-interval coefficient package. So a broader regulator-stability theorem cannot possibly be a theorem of raw coefficient equality.
Corollary 1 (Exact leading support-prototype benchmark). Let $R,S$ be any support pair whose first visible exchange order is generated by the leading coefficients. Then $$ \frac{E_{R,S}^{(\lambda)}(\Delta)-I}{c_\lambda^2\Delta^4} = [A_R^{(1)},A_S^{(1)}]+O(\Delta^2), $$ and after bond-centered spatial renormalization the entire admissible $\lambda$-family shares the same leading tangential operator identified in phase 3.
Proof. The finite-lattice renormalized defect expansion follows by inserting Proposition 1 into the exact group-commutator expansion for the first visible exchange order. The common bond-centered tangential operator is exactly the phase-3 leading-order universality theorem. $ \square $
Remark. Corollary 1 is the first genuine stability benchmark across a nontrivial regulator family. It shows exactly what “same continuum law” can mean here: not the same microscopic coefficient, but the same bond-centered operator after onset normalization.

III. Normalized strip-moment stability across support prototypes

Phase 4 identified the right higher-order invariant: first strip moments of the exact strip-channel coefficients. The regulator-stability question should therefore be posed directly at that level.

Definition 1 (Regulator-dependent coefficient families). Let $\alpha$ label an admissible regulator family. For a support type $\sigma$ and order $p\ge 1$, let $A_{\sigma,n,\alpha}^{(p)}$ denote the order-$\Delta^{2p}$ coefficient attached to the translate of type $\sigma$ centered at site $n$ for regulator $\alpha$. Define $$ \mathcal K_{\sigma,a,\alpha}^{(p)}[N] := a\sum_n N_nA_{\sigma,n,\alpha}^{(p)}. $$
Hypothesis 1 (Regulator-wise strip closure with common channel identification). Fix support types $\sigma,\tau$ and orders $p,q$. Assume that for every regulator $\alpha$ in the class:
  1. there exists a common identified finite channel family $X_{n+1/2}^{(\ell,\chi)}$ such that $$ [A_{\sigma,n,\alpha}^{(p)},A_{\tau,m,\alpha}^{(q)}] = \sum_{(\ell,\chi)} c_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)}(m-n)\, X_{n+1/2}^{(\ell,\chi)}; $$
  2. the coefficient functions are finitely supported and antisymmetric under $(\sigma,p,n,\alpha)\leftrightarrow(\tau,q,m,\alpha)$;
  3. each channel family is bond-centered admissible in the sense of phase 4.
Definition 2 (Normalized first strip moments). Let $\rho_{\sigma,\alpha}^{(p)}>0$ be regulator-dependent normalization factors. For each active channel $(\ell,\chi)$, define the normalized first strip moment by $$ \widehat C_{\sigma,\tau}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)} := \frac{1}{\rho_{\sigma,\alpha}^{(p)}\rho_{\tau,\alpha}^{(q)}} \sum_r r\,c_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)}(r), $$ whenever the right-hand side is independent of $\alpha$ across the regulator class.
Conditional Theorem 1 (Normalized strip-moment regulator stability across support prototypes). Assume Hypothesis 1 and assume that the normalized first strip moments of Definition 2 are regulator-independent across the class. Then for every smooth compactly supported two-component profile $\phi$, $$ \widehat{\mathfrak E}_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q)}[N,M]\iota_a\phi = \iota_a\bigl(\widehat K_{\parallel;\sigma,\tau}^{(p,q)}[\beta]\phi\bigr)+O(a), $$ where $$ \widehat{\mathfrak E}_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q)}[N,M] := \frac{ [\mathcal K_{\sigma,a,\alpha}^{(p)}[N],\mathcal K_{\tau,a,\alpha}^{(q)}[M]] }{ \rho_{\sigma,\alpha}^{(p)}\rho_{\tau,\alpha}^{(q)} }, $$ $$ \beta=N\partial_xM-M\partial_xN, $$ and $$ \widehat K_{\parallel;\sigma,\tau}^{(p,q)}[\beta] := \sum_{(\ell,\chi)} \widehat C_{\sigma,\tau}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)}K_{\ell,\chi}[\beta]. $$ In particular, the renormalized bond-centered continuum operator is regulator-independent across the prototype class.
Proof. Apply the phase-4 strip-moment reduction theorem regulator by regulator. For each $\alpha$ one obtains $$ [\mathcal K_{\sigma,a,\alpha}^{(p)}[N],\mathcal K_{\tau,a,\alpha}^{(q)}[M]]\iota_a\phi = \sum_{(\ell,\chi)} C_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)} \mathcal T_a^{(\ell,\chi)}[\beta]\iota_a\phi +O(a), $$ with $$ C_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)} = \sum_r r\,c_{\sigma,\tau,\alpha}^{(p,q;\ell,\chi)}(r). $$ Divide by $\rho_{\sigma,\alpha}^{(p)}\rho_{\tau,\alpha}^{(q)}$ and use the regulator-independence of the normalized moments. The common continuum operator is then exactly the displayed sum of the common normalized moments multiplying the common channel actions $K_{\ell,\chi}[\beta]$. $ \square $
Corollary 2 (The exact $\lambda$-family leading benchmark is the first realized case). For interval support pairs whose first visible exchange order is generated by the leading coefficients, Corollary 1 is the case $$ \rho_{R,\lambda}^{(1)}=c_\lambda, \qquad \rho_{S,\lambda}^{(1)}=c_\lambda, $$ and the common normalized strip moments are exactly the leading boundary-packet moments identified in phases 2 and 3.
Remark. The theorem says exactly what broader regulator stability should mean. It is not a theorem that all admissible regulators have the same microscopic coefficient algebra. It is a theorem that after identifying the common strip channels and dividing out the regulator-dependent onset data, the surviving first strip moments are the same. That is the correct transition-law invariant.

IV. The Barandes perspective on regulator stability

The temptation at this stage is to ask whether some preferred microscopic representation or some extra ontological postulate will restore raw universality. That would be the wrong lesson. The exact localized transition law has already told us what survives and what does not survive. Raw coefficients do not survive. Normalized strip moments may.

So the right notion of stability is operational and algebraic. One asks whether different admissible regulators, viewed as different localized regularizations of the same finite deformation problem, produce the same bond-centered tangential action once the regulator-dependent onset factors have been removed. If they do, that common action has been earned by the exact transition-law algebra itself. If they do not, then the difference is physical at the level of the present axioms and should not be hidden behind continuum rhetoric.

Remark. This is the same Barandes-style moral as before, now at broader prototype level: do not elevate representational conveniences above the transition law. Stability, if it exists, has to be read off from the exact stochastic-comparison structure.

V. What phase 5 now closes and what it defers

With the scope fixed this way, phase 5 is complete in the following exact sense.

  1. Exact rejection of raw prototype-level universality. The admissible $\lambda$-family already rules out raw regulator-independent leading coefficients across interval prototypes.
  2. Exact leading benchmark. The phase-3 $\lambda$-family result is placed explicitly as the first nontrivial support-prototype stability test.
  3. Correct broader theorem. The normalized strip-moment criterion is isolated as the right regulator-stability statement beyond the leading benchmark.
  4. Proper invariant identified. The relevant cross-regulator datum is the normalized strip-moment package, not raw matrix equality.

What remains deliberately outside the present claim is equally important.

  1. Explicit higher-order moment computations beyond the exact leading benchmark. Those still need to be carried out for concrete regulator families.
  2. Explicit cross-regulator comparison beyond the $\lambda$-family. The theorem gives the criterion, not yet the full family-by-family calculations.
  3. Global ontology-expanding phases. Variable particle number, gauge coupling, and detector/control dynamics remain downstream of the unresolved higher-order comparison work.
Strategic remark. This completes phase 5 at the strongest honest scope now available. The paper does not pretend that broader cross-regulator stability has been explicitly computed. It states exactly what broader stability would have to mean and what algebraic data must be matched for such a theorem to be true.

VI. Conclusion

Phase 5 is now complete at the strongest exact-and-conditional scope supported by the current relativistic ISP stack. The exact lesson is already decisive: raw prototype-level regulator universality fails. The positive lesson is equally decisive: the right candidate invariant is the normalized strip-moment package of the bond-centered exchange algebra.

That is the right outcome from the Barandes point of view. One neither overclaims microscopic universality nor gives up on structural stability. One asks what the exact transition law really preserves after the correct normalization, and one carries only that preserved structure forward. The next remaining burden is therefore explicit rather than conceptual: compute the non-leading strip moments for concrete prototype and regulator families and see when the criterion is actually realized.
